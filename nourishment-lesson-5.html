Nourishment

So, What Should You Eat?

If the IC Diet is out, but food is still a very important part of healing, then what do I recommend you eat? I’m so glad you asked!

Just because a food isn’t acidic, or a “known irritant”, doesn’t mean it’s OK to consume while you’re healing. Let’s try to move away from that idea altogether. Instead, I want you to start thinking about whole foods.

Whole foods is a term that has been used so loosely in wellness culture that it has almost lost its meaning. In the context of Integrative Healing, it means foods that exist as close as possible to the way nature made them: not processed, not refined, not fortified with synthetic nutrients, not stripped of their natural fat and rebuilt with stabilizers.

This is a deliberately different way of eating from what most health and wellness culture recommends. The goal here is not low-fat, low-carb, high protein, or high-fiber. We’re not eliminating dairy and grains. In fact, we’re flipping the “health food” culture on its head. It might sound out to lunch to those of you who’ve been big on healthy eating for a long time, but hear me out.

Healthy carbohydrates and dairy are part of this approach and are not the enemy. Jasmine rice, oats, and sourdough bread are stabilizing, nourishing foods that give the body fuel for healing. Cutting carbohydrates makes healing harder in most cases, not easier. Getting off processed foods and their associated load of preservatives, artificial colours, seed oils, and synthetic additives is equally important, as these place additional burden on a detoxification system that is already working hard.

When healing IC, which statement is true?

A

Raw dairy and sourdough bread can be nourishing and stabilizing parts of a healing diet.

B

Dairy and gluten should be eliminated — they are inflammatory and hard on the gut.

That’s right. Raw dairy and properly fermented sourdough behave very differently in the body than processed versions. They are stabilizing, nourishing, and well-tolerated by most people who are healing.
Actually, dairy and gluten are not the problem here. Raw dairy and sourdough bread are stabilizing, nourishing foods that support healing. It’s the processing that causes issues — not the food itself.

Question 2 of 3

Which of these is a better choice while your bladder is healing?

A

A green smoothie with spinach, kale, and almond milk.

B

A smoothie made with milk, banana, and maple syrup.

A smoothie made with milk and banana gives you healing enzymes, gentle oxalate support, and a balance of carbohydrate and fat that actually nourishes. Pair it with a salad of lettuce, cucumber, and carrot topped with chicken for a complete meal.
Spinach, kale, and almonds are extremely high in oxalates — some of the worst choices for a healing bladder, even though they contain healthy nutrients.

The IC Diet is actually relatively easy to follow — that’s not the problem. The real issue is that it manages symptoms without healing anything. So you can follow it perfectly and still stay stuck in the cycle of painful flares.

Overnight Oats Three Ways

Base Ingredients

½ cup rolled oats, raw milk or water, honey or maple syrup, pinch of salt.

Mix and chill in an airtight jar. In the morning, add your toppings.

Toppings

Banana & Honey → sliced banana + drizzle of honey

Blueberry Delight → fresh/frozen blueberries + splash of maple syrup

Apple Crisp → diced apple + maple syrup and pinch of nutmeg (skip cinnamon)

Feel free to try other combinations of low-oxalate fruit, raw dairy, natural sweetener and pinches of well-tolerated spices like nutmeg.

Banana Smoothie

Ingredients

  • 1 cup of homemade yogurt or raw milk
  • ½ cup of ice
  • 1 banana
  • 1 teaspoon of honey (optional)
  • Add-in: blueberries, strawberries, mango or other low oxalate fruit

Directions

  1. In a blender, add one ripe banana, ½ cup of raw milk or homemade yogurt, a handful of ice, a small drizzle of honey, and any low oxalate fruit.
  2. Blend on high until smooth and creamy.
  3. Taste and adjust sweetness if needed.
  4. Pour into a glass and serve immediately.

To turn this into a smoothie bowl, use ½ cup raw milk, ½ cup homemade yogurt and pour into a bowl. Top with dried coconut, maple-toasted oats, and/or low oxalate fruits like berries or peaches.

Carrot-Cucumber Salad with Creamy Avocado-Mayo Dressing

Ingredients

  • 1 heaping cup chopped iceberg or romaine lettuce
  • ¼ cup shredded carrot
  • ¼ cup peeled cucumber
  • 1–2 tbsp raw cheese, grated or crumbled

Dressing

  • ¼ ripe avocado
  • 1 tablespoon homemade mayo
  • Pinch of Celtic or “Real” Salt
  • 1–2 tsp filtered water to thin, if needed

Directions

  1. Blend dressing ingredients until creamy.
  2. Toss lettuce, carrot, and cucumber in a bowl.
  3. Drizzle with dressing and top with raw cheese.

Optional Protein Add-On: Top with 2–3 oz sliced chicken or turkey for a full lunch version.

Maple Glazed Salmon

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon olive oil
  • 6 3–4 ounce salmon fillets
  • Salt and white pepper
  • 1/3 cup real maple syrup
  • 2 tablespoons San-J “No Soy” Tamari Sauce (or coconut aminos)
  • 1 clove garlic*, finely minced
  • 1 teaspoon grated fresh ginger
  • Fresh chives for garnish (optional)
*Garlic is a sulfur food. Omit this for a lower sulfur meal.

Directions

  1. Preheat your oven to 375 degrees F.
  2. Heat a large cast iron over medium-high heat and add the oil.
  3. Season the salmon fillets on both sides with salt and pepper and add to the hot pan, top side down.
  4. Let brown until caramel-coloured on the first side (about 2 minutes) and then flip to brown on the other side.
  5. While the salmon is browning, whisk together the maple syrup, coconut aminos, fresh grated garlic, and ginger in a small bowl and pour it over the salmon fillets.
  6. Add the whole pan to the preheated oven, uncovered, and cook for about 7–10 minutes, or until the salmon is cooked through and flakes with a fork.
  7. Spoon the maple glaze over the salmon again before serving. Garnish with chopped chives, if desired. Serve with jasmine rice and low oxalate vegetable.
