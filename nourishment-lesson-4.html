Nourishment

Ammonia

Certain genetic variants cause the body to process sulfur compounds in a way that produces ammonia as a byproduct.

That ammonia ends up concentrated in the urine, where it irritates the bladder lining (and, more specifically, the urethra) and produces a burning sensation that is frequently mistaken for infection or a general flare.

When the bladder lining is already being torn up by oxalate crystals, which are acidic in their own right, the ammonia landing on that tissue is roughly analogous to pouring battery acid on an open wound.

If your IC was anything like mine was, you don’t have to imagine what that feels like. You’ve probably been living it.

How genetics play a role

Two genetic variations worth mentioning.

Many IC sufferers share one (or both) of these two genetic variations that affect the way their bodies produce excess ammonia.

Variant 1

CBS

Think of CBS as a gate in your body’s chemistry. Normally that gate opens just enough to let a controlled amount of sulfur through for processing. When the gate is working correctly, the sulfur is processed cleanly and safely.

Variant 2

A1298C

A1298C affects your body’s recycling function. Your body is supposed to take a compound called homocysteine and recycle it back into something useful. A1298C means that recycling process runs slowly or poorly, causing homocysteine to build up and putting extra pressure on the CBS gate.

It’s really not critical that you fully understand this process. It’s enough just to know that genetics play an important role in how your body processes sulfur, and that a large number of people with IC have these genetic variants.

Until you decide to do more testing to find out for sure, it’s best to gradually reduce sulfur foods until you’re consuming no more than 2 servings a week.
