Nourishment

What Oxalates Actually Do

Oxalates are naturally occurring compounds found in plants. In small amounts, a healthy gut handles them easily and eliminates them through the digestive tract.

How the problem unfolds — tap each step

The Gut tap to expand ⌄

The problem arises when the gut is compromised, as it is in virtually everyone with IC, and oxalates are absorbed into the bloodstream instead of being excreted normally.

The Bloodstream tap to expand ⌄

Once in the bloodstream, oxalates travel through the body and are filtered out by the kidneys into the urine. This is where they cause damage for IC sufferers.

The Bladder tap to expand ⌄

Oxalate crystals in the urine are sharp and abrasive, and they drag across a bladder lining that is already compromised, tearing at tissue that is trying to heal. Every high-oxalate meal is, in effect, sandpapering the inside of your bladder. Yes, ouch.

This looks like a healthy lunch — and it’s perfectly fine on the IC Diet. But if you’re struggling with bladder pain, spinach and almonds are among the very worst things you could eat.

But I’m sure your doctor filled you in on this. No? Your naturopath? Hmm.

High-oxalate foods

Foods many people consider healthy.

High-oxalate foods that many people consider healthy include spinach, almonds and almond-based products, kale, Swiss chard, beets, chocolate, sweet potatoes, and many nuts and seeds. Smoothies packed with spinach and almond milk are among the worst possible choices for someone with IC, and yet many IC sufferers are encouraged to enjoy them daily. It’s insanity.

Reducing oxalate load is one of the most immediate and impactful dietary shifts you can make.
